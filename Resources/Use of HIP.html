Easy access to data-based knowledge and information in HIP takes place in two ways, partly by visualization of measurements, model calculations and statistical information about hydrological conditions near the surface, and partly by providing easy and unified access to free public hydrological data within a geographical area, e.g. a river basin or a municipality. Briefly about the use of data from HIP Data visualization in HIP will support dialogue and cooperation across sectors and administrative boundaries, as well as with citizens and advisors on climate adaptation, water management and other planning, where extreme water volumes are important. For example, it may be urban and infrastructure planning or prioritisation of areas for sewer renovation, environmental protection, agricultural production, set-aside of areas for forest and recreational purposes and much more that is reflected in different user stories. Data in HIP can be downloaded both via web services or downloaded. In this way, comprehensive access to data and model calculations on off- road hydrological conditions support both the management of public authorities and companies’ development of local solutions and new IT tools that can be used for climate adaptation and climate protection. Read more about data documentation. Data and model calculations in HIP cannot stand alone. On the other hand, both measurements and model calculations are subject to uncertainties that need to be reserved for, and on the other hand, there are many factors other than water that have an impact on climate adaptation and climate protection, e.g. people and the environment. In HIP there are user manuals to help assess the uncertainty and suitability/validity of the model calculations in relation to different applications. A selection of HIP model calculations can also be seen in KAMP at www.klimatilpasning.dk together with other data that are important for use in climate adaptation, such as environmental, nature and plan and property data. Use of HIP for screening Visualization of measurements and model calculations in HIP (hipdata.dk) can be used for screening problem areas and support discussion of solutions that can be placed strategic places in the landscape, e.g. options for temporary storage of water or other solutions. The identified possibilities must then be examined in more detail, often with the use of additional data, as well as assessed in relation to possible consequences for e.g. the environment, production, cultural values and social considerations locally or elsewhere in the catchment area. It requires insight into the entire water cycle and assessment of risks of flooding from both extreme rain, streams, off- road groundwater and storm surges to identify holistic and cost- effective solutions that can help create a safe and sustainable future for the benefit of all. It is important to inform yourself of the uncertainty of model calculations to assess whether the calculations in a given area meet accuracy criteria for application at screening level. Model uncertainty is on display on map in HIP. Using HIP for retail planning HIP includes a new unique opportunity to download data and model calculations including so-called rim conditions for local analyses and further development of local models and more detailed mappings that can be used for retail planning. Access to peripheral conditions in HIP creates a common frame of reference for the further development of local models, decision support tools and mappings that are consistent and coherent across geographic regions. Rand conditions should be used with additional relevant local and more detailed data, e.g. more geological data, watercourse measurements, data on fastening, sewers, sinks and drains, or data from drones, radar, lidar and other remote sensing data providing information on hydrological conditions. More data allows for more accurate and more detailed mappings in e.g. 10 or 20 m grid. Rand conditions can also be used to develop warning systems that take groundwater dynamics into account and include real-time data from IoT sensors. Rand conditions in HIP include both climate scenarios as well as dynamic and stationary (period-mediated) rim conditions for a historical (reference) period (1990-2019) as well as for the future (2041-2070 and 2071-2100) for a wide range of conditions (groundwater potential, depth for groundwater, water flow (watercourses), infiltration to saturated zone, horizontal groundwater flow and vertical exchange with deeper layers), and download of the hydrostratigraphic model, model insecurity. The application of peripheral conditions requires expert insight into both data and methods, including knowledge of methods to work with climate scenarios. An expert guide for use in the work with rim conditions can be found her <#toc-6>here. Data and model calculations can be downloaded via the HIP user interface for stream catchment areas, municipalities and for 10 km squared areas (rim conditions) selected using a polygon drawing tool in HIP (hipdata.dk). HIP includes maps, time series and statistical calculations for the depths for off-road groundwater, stream data, the soil water content and sea level in coastal zones, both measurements and model calculations. Terrain-nutritious groundwater is defined as the first free groundwater mirror one encounters from above (including hanging groundwater mirror). In practice, it is assumed that bearings of groundwater from boreholes with intake within 10 m below ground are supposed to give a correct picture of off-road groundwater, however, a concrete assessment should be made in each case. Measurements can be found from about 1900 to today. The model calculations include daily calculations in the period 1/1-1990 to 31/12-2019, as well as model-calculated forecasts for the future, showing how the depth to off-road groundwater and water flow in streams is expected to change. The availability of data in HIP is described under Documentation. This section describes how data is displayed in HIP. How to view historical data, real-time data and forecasts for the future? In HIP, data on off-preach hydrological conditions is displayed in historical times, in real time and for the future under three separate tabs. Historical measurements and model calculations are found in a historic tab in HIP, real-time measurements are found in a real-time tab, and model-calculated forecasts for the near (2041-2070) and distant (2071-2100) futures are found in a future tab. There is a bit of overlap between measurements in the historical tab and the real-time tab. In the Historical tab, all measurements appear as far back as the beginning of the 1900s to the present day, while the real- time tab only displays data from measuring stations that are active on the day you use HIP. Thus, in the real-time tab, the most recently measured value from active measuring stations is displayed. When choosing a measuring station with the mouse, you will see measurements from the last week until and including today's date in a graph, so that you can follow the current ongoing development. This may be relevant, for example, in cases of rising water levels and risk of flooding or other risks. What is the significance of the colors used to display data? Data is generally displayed with a colour gamut from blue to red, where blue indicates “most water” (“wet”) or “more water” (effect of climate change), and red indicates “least water” (i.e. a relatively “dry” situation) or “minor water” (effect of climate change). For example, a map of _the depth for off-road groundwater_ will show areas with low depth to groundwater with blue colors (groundwater close to terrain), whereas the deeper (> 10 m) is displayed with a dark red color. See Figure 1. _Groundwater level (DVR90)_ is shown correspondingly with a blue-red scale, where blue is the lowest cots close to the average surface of the ocean (coast, river valleys, lows), and the highest cots are seen with dark red color. *Figure* 1. Modelusikkerhed Modelberegningernes usikkerhed er beregnet ud fra sammenligning med tilgængelige målinger i 3 forskellige perioder 1990-1999, 2000-2010 og 2011-2019. De beregnede usikkerheder vises med farver på kort i HIP, og de anvendte kalibrerings- og valideringsdata, der har været brugt til at foretage beregningene kan downloades fra HIP sammen med de beregnede modelusikkerheder. Modelberegningerne er kalibreret og valideret med brug af data fra terrænnære grundsvandsboringer samt målinger af vandføring i vandløb. Der har været anvendt maskinlæring til at identificere de terrænnære grundvandsboringer, der har data, der er egnede til hydrologisk modellering. Usikkerhed for terrænnært grundvand Der udstilles tre usikkerhedskort for hhv. 1990-1999, 2000-2010 og 2011-2019, der viser middelfejlen (i meter) på modelberegninger i forhold til tilgængelige målinger (pejlinger) i en boring. Middelfejlen (ME) beskriver den gennemsnitlige afvigelse mellem observerede og modellerede dybder til det terrænnære grundvand i den pågældende periode: * /En positiv ME:/ indikerer at de modelberegnede dybder til det terrænnære grundvand generelt overestimeres (altså står tættere på terræn) end de observerede dybder til det terrænnære grundvand. * /En ME omkring 0:/ indikerer at de modelberegnede dybder til det terrænnære grundvand generelt ikke har afvigelser i forhold til de observerede dybder til det terrænnære grundvand. * /En negativ ME:/ indikerer at de modelberegnede dybder til det terrænnære grundvand generelt underestimeres (altså står længere fra terræn) end de observerede dybder til det terrænnære grundvand Middelfejlen er beregnet med brug af kvalitetssikrede målinger (pejlinger) med et indtag indenfor 10 meter under terræn. Der er anvendt maskinlæring til at identificere egnede terrænnære grundvandsdata (pejlinger) til hydrologisk modellering. Dette er beskrevet i dokumentationsrapporten . Middelfejlen er i størstedelen af landet mellem +/- 2 m, hvilket er acceptabelt for terrænnært grundvand (defineret som det første frie grundvandsspejl man støder på fra oven inden for 10 m under terræn). I HIP vises små middelfejl med en dæmpet grå farve (dvs. hvor der er god overensstemmelse med observationer), hvorimod de større middelfejl vises med tydeligere blå (overestimeret dybde) og røde (underestimeret dybde) farver. Usikkerhed for vandføring (vandløb) Usikkerhed for vandføring i vandløb visualiseres med brug af en række parametre, der beskriver overensstemmelsen mellem observeret og modelleret vandføring i de samme 3 perioder som for terrænnært grundvand, 1990-1999, 2000-2010 og 2011-2019: * Kling-Glupta Effiency (KGE) - der beskriver modellernes evne til at simulere vandføringsdynamikken i den valgte periode, altså hvor godt variationerne i vandføring simuleres. * Vandbalancefejlen (WBE) - der er et mål for afvigelsen i % mellem observeret og modelleret vandføring i den valgte periode, altså hvor godt vandbalancen simuleres. * Maksimumsafstrømningen (Q01) - der er et mål for afvigelsen i % mellem observeret og simuleret maksimumsvandføring i den valgte periode, altså hvor godt maksimumværdier simuleres. Alle tre mål er visualiseret i forhold til, om de vurderes at opfylde kriterier for anvendelse på screeningsniveau. I alt anvendes 4 kategorier til at vise om modelberegninger er egnede til detailanalyser (blå), overslagsberegninger (grøn), screening (gul), eller om de er for usikre til at anvendes til screening (rød). Before you use the model calculations On the Hydrological Information and Forecast System you can access a number of model calculations for the terrain hydrological conditions developed with the DK model (vandmodel.dk ). The DK model is based on a simplistic 3-dimensional description of real (physical) conditions that are relevant to the freshwater cycle. Therefore, on the basis of a number of national data on climate, geology, soil, watercourse, land use, groundwater, groundwater, drinking water abstraction, irrigation, sea water level, etc., is able to produce nationwide model calculations that provide a model-calculated estimate of the depth to the terrain-near groundwater, the water flow in streams, the soil water content and other conditions linked to the freshwater circuit. By using measurements of precipitation and other climate data back in time as input to the model, you can get a nationwide overview of where groundwater _has expected_ to _have been_ in the period in question and how it develops. On the other hand, if you use suggestions as input on how precipitation will change as a result of climate change, you can get a nationwide overview of where groundwater _is expected to be in the future_ as a result of climate change. Input data on climate is available from global and regional climate models. In HIP, calculations from 22 climate models have been used for climate development in Denmark. Model calculations are therefore a good supplement to measurements, as it can provide nationwide overview of the terrain-near hydrological conditions. In addition, model calculations can also help to elucidate what changes we can expect in the terrain-near hydrological conditions as a result of climate change. The model calculations on Hydrological Information and Forecast System contribute to a nationwide screening basis for the terrain hydrological conditions (with the exception of Læsø, Endelave, Tunø, Samsø, Anholt, Æbleø, Vejrø and Christiansø). How do I assess the uncertainty of model calculations? A model is rarely 100% correct, and there will be specific areas where you need to pay special attention to the fact that the model may have difficulty producing model calculations that reproduce the actual conditions. Therefore, when using the model calculations, it is always necessary to consider whether the model is able to reproduce the actual conditions in the given area. Such an assessment is facilitated by comparing the results of the model with actual measurements, local knowledge and data. For example, on the user interface, it is possible to visualize 'uncertificate' on selected model calculations. The uncertainty is calculated by comparing model calculations and actual measurements. Nothing can be said in general about the areas in which the model is particularly uncertain, but it should be taken into account at least that the model is set up, calibrated and validated with a focus on consistency and uniformity across the country. All types of anthropogenic influences of the terrain conditions therefore provide a special uncertainty, whether it is infrastructure, changed drainage conditions at transport facilities, in cities or other conditions that may affect the off-road hydrological conditions. Special vigilance should be directed at: * Urban areas. To a certain extent, the model takes into account for fortified areas, but assumptions regarding drainage, evaporation, pipe laying of streams or LAR that affect the groundwater mirror locally, the model does not take into account. The model assumes that rainwater drains to the nearest stream. * Complex geology. The model has a resolution of 100 m, which means that local variations are not included in DK model HIP. However, more data is included for the scaling down of off-road groundwater for 10 m grid with the use of machine learning * Moraine clay areas or other areas with continuous clay layers in the upper meters, where representativeness of bearings may be less good. Here one should be more careful to assume that a bearing drilling filter sat in the top 10 m is representative of the depth for off- road groundwater (phreatic depth). A filter just below a clay layer can represent a deeper aquifer. * Streams and the model's limited ability to simulate backflow and stowage. During periods of large runoff, there may be delay in bridge throughput and in floods of adjacent areas which in theory can affect the simulation of maximum runoffs. At the same time, measurements in such situations are less reliable. * Although the model is not hydrodynamic, at prolonged storm surge events, tripods in stream systems may affect the water balance. The model describes the prolonged sea level rise, but storm surge events have been disregarded in both historical and advanced periods. * The model represents relatively simple assumptions regarding drainage systems and in cases where the water mirror is regulated artificially (controlled drainage, pumping plants, groundwater lowering, etc.). * The model assumes the same land use and the same crop throughout the historical period, and this may give an impact on the groundwater, although the overall importance is assessed limited in relation to other factors. * Phrases of low-floor areas and other areas are not included with a dynamic description in the model. The above is not an exhaustive list, but can help in the localization of areas where there is a risk of particularly high uncertainties on the model calculations. In areas with measurements, it will probably be reflected in the uncertainties if an area has conditions out of the ordinary - while in areas without measurements, it is not possible to assess the uncertainty. In general, therefore, if data are not available in an area, it is not possible to document whether the model is valid. Production of peripheral conditions All rim condition data are provided based on simulation results with ‘DK-model HIP’. ‘DK model HIP’ is in model setup, calibration, simulations and uncertainty assessment focused against simulation of depth to off-road groundwater, water content in the root zone and water flow in streams. Here is the use without use in a local model, but may well include a quantification of e.g. water volumes flowing to streams, lake or coastline. * Numerical i.e. as pressure level or possibly flux rim conditions for local simulations with local model. In the case of the latter, there are a myriad of possible uses. For example, it may be as flux (or flow) input for a hydrodynamic calculation of water levels on a watercourse stretch, where the water flow upstream and inflows is retrieved and used for calculating water mirror down through the water flow. Another use could be in the form of lower pressure levels rim to Daisy or other model of unsaturated zone that simulates e.g. crop production or leaching of nitrate, pesticides, etc. A third application can be the rim conditions for an urban model, and fourth a classic hydrological model as a submodel, where e.g. pressure level rim conditions in all layers at the edge of the 10 submodels are drawn from the DK model HIP 500m model. When using peripheral conditions in connection with the conceptual level (I) it is important that you are aware at all times that model calculations (like measurements) have some uncertainty. The uncertainty on the model calculations is seen in HIP and by comparing the model calculations with actual measurements of the depth for terrain local water and water flow are in streams. It would always be a good idea to include local knowledge and possibly local data that is not found in e.g. Jupiter, and including these in connection with the assessment of the manner in which data from the model can be used in an optimal way as a basis for a screening or initial assessment. When using peripheral conditions numerically for a local model (II), there are a number of considerations to be made, and it will typically be part of the establishment of a conceptual model that these considerations are described. Questions such as where to place the rim shall delineate the outer boundary of the local model. See e.g. Hydrological Geoguide 2017/1 on the establishment of conceptual model (Henriksen et al., 2017) or Chapter 7 “Determining of Randing Conditions” from Handbook in groundwater modelling (Sonnenborg & Henriksen, 2005/80) on detailed information about the use and choice of rim conditions. With HIP, pressure level (or flux) can be downloaded for any grid and it allows for far greater flexibility than if the position of the rim were to be tuned to the measuring points (watering stations, point of reference, etc.). You also don’t have to hunt good ‘no-flow’ circles to the same extent. On the other hand, consideration should be given to whether the depth of distribution can be delineated to include primarily the groundwater and the top underlying regional avaluating layer. It depends entirely on the purpose of the task. Hydrological geoguide (Henriksen et al. 2017) is a good starting point for the conditions included in the installation of a conceptual understanding model (level I), and there is also for hydrological models and groundwater models a lot of guidance that can be useful when the application is numerical (level II). If you work with the entire freshwater circuit, it may be advantageous to use model layers structured in the same calculation layers as in 'DK-model HIP'. If you need a greater detail of the most off-road parts, you can choose to use the pressure level from aquifer layers in the local model, and thus increase the discrepancy if needed, and simply use no-flow rim conditioning for low-permeable clay layers, etc. It will be advantageous to have a certain buffer zone outside the area of interest in a local model, but no general recommendations can be made to how big such should be. In HIP it is possible to view different data including model uncertainties for calibration and validation periods on cards that can be used as background maps for selection of areas you want to download the peripheral conditions for and continue working with, with the use of additional local data. As DK model HIPs are set up and calibrated with a focus on the off-road hydrological conditions, it must be made subject to the fact that there may be greater uncertainties on model calculations from deeper layers in the DK model. In towns/close settlements, at larger transport facilities and in areas close to the coast or areas with complex geological conditions, uncertainty on model-calculated horizontal groundwater flow may also be greater. The use of the model calculations as a peripheral conditions must always be assessed individually for any task in relation to the nature of the task as well as the details and precision required. Data should be used in conjunction with other available data. Choice of climate models There are no general rules or recommendations for how many sets of climate scenarios/climate projections to be used to assess the effects of climate change or how to choose climate models. The best thing is to use all climate projections and use the results to assess the uncertainty, but this can be very calculated and impossible in practice. In general, however, the more climate models one bases the calculations on, the better representation of uncertainty on the impact of climate change. Initially, you have to decide which emission scenario you want to base your analysis on. The choice of emission scenario is dependent on planning horizons and requirements for robustness. This is explained by DMI in collaboration with the Danish Environmental Protection Agency in “Guidance in the use of emissions scenarios”, which can be seen here . If you only have the opportunity to use a limited number of climate models for your analysis, you can be inspired by a number of examples described in the following: In an assessment of the impact of climate change on the aquatic environment in relation to changes in groundwater levels, three climate models were chosen to represent a “median” climate model, which covers models that are “central” in the pool of climate models, a “wet” climate model that simulates a large increase in precipitation, as well as a “dry” climate model that simulates a large increase in evaporation (Henriksen et al. Figure 2 shows an example of variation in simulated climate change from 16 climate models with a high (RCP 8.5) emission scenario that forms the basis for the choice of climate model. Simulated climate change is being seen on an annual basis as well as for the periods of April-September and October-March to choose the median, wet and dry climate model. In an analysis of the effect of climate change on groundwater formation, 3 climate models were also chosen according to simple ranking, on the one hand, a median model and partly the wettest and driest model (Henriksen et al. (2012). In an analysis of the effect of climate change on leaching pesticides, two climate models were chosen (Henriksen et al. 2013). One model was chosen because it was well in line with observations during the reference period and because the future calculations for this climate model represented a “between level” for all the climate models. The second model was chosen because it had a stronger climate change signal (both temperature and precipitation), which allowed to examine the range of climate change's effect. In a project dealing with climate protection of built-up areas and roads, 9 climate models (Randall et al., 2013; Troldborg & Refsgaard, 2019) were used. The median change of the nine climate models is then used to estimate the dimensional groundwater mirror, while the spread between the nine models is used as a measure of the uncertainty (Troldborg & Refsgard, 2019). The choice of a limited number of climate models should also take into account the purpose of the analysis. For example, if you want to assess the risk of flooding from a “worst-case” consideration, then you can choose to use the most “wet” climate models.
